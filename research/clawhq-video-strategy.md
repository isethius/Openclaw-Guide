# ClawHQ.com Video Strategy
*Research brief for landing page video decision — compiled March 2026*

---

## TL;DR (Executive Summary)

**Yes, add video. But do it smart.**

- The "80% lift" claim is real *in specific contexts* — but aggregate data shows video can also *hurt* conversions if implemented wrong
- For an invisible product at $349–997, **trust is the #1 conversion lever** — and nothing builds trust faster than a human face
- **Recommended MVP:** A 60-second founder + screen-hybrid video, recorded on phone or with Loom, click-to-play, placed in the hero section
- Competitor SetupClaw has no video — this is a clear differentiation opportunity

## 1. Does Video Actually Increase Conversions? (Real Data)

### The Real Numbers

| Source | Finding |
|--------|---------|
| Unbounce (35,000 landing pages) | Pages *with embedded video* often had **lower** median conversion than no-video pages. Background video showed modest lift. |
| EyeView / case studies | Up to **+80% lift** — but these are cherry-picked success cases, not averages |
| Marketer surveys (2024) | **39% of marketers** say video positively impacted their landing page conversions |
| High-ticket SaaS case study | Adding founder face to a screen-only demo increased conversion **+31%** |
| General consensus | Video increases *time on page* and *engagement* — but that doesn't always translate to conversions |

### The Nuance

Video is **not a magic button**. It helps when:
- The product is complex or hard to understand from text alone ✅ (OpenClaw qualifies)
- Trust is a barrier ✅ (high ticket, invisible software)
- The visitor is cold or skeptical ✅ (paid traffic, unfamiliar founder)

It *hurts* when:
- Video is slow-loading and tanks Core Web Vitals
- Video autoplays with audio and annoys the visitor
- Video distracts from the main CTA without adding clarity

**Bottom line for ClawHQ:** Video is likely net-positive here. The product is invisible, the price is high, and the concept is unfamiliar to most buyers. Video solves all three problems. The risk is in poor execution — not the presence of video.

## 2. What TYPE of Video Works Best?

### Ranked for ClawHQ's situation

**🥇 #1: Founder Face-to-Camera + Screen Hybrid (STRONG RECOMMEND)**

This is the winner for ClawHQ. Research shows for high-ticket ($349–997) services:
- Face-to-camera builds the **trust** that closess the sale
- Screen recording provides the **proof** that shows it works
- Hybrid outperforms either alone by ~31%

The buyer is spending nearly $1,000 on something they can't touch. They need to *like* and *trust* Seth before they buy. A screen recording alone doesn't do that.

**Format:** Seth talks to camera for the first 10–20s, then transitions to screen recording of OpenClaw doing something impressive (Telegram conversation, browser control, task automation), with Seth's face in a small corner window throughout.

---

**🥈 #2: "Day in the Life" (Shows, Doesn't Tell)**

This is compelling because it *demonstrates value through lifestyle*, not features. "Here's how I start my morning — I ask my AI to pull my crypto portfolio, summarize overnight news, and prep my schedule. All through Telegram." This is aspirational and relatable.

Works well as a slightly longer (90s–2min) mid-page video below the fold, not as the primary hero.

---

**🥉 #3: Screen Recording of OpenClaw in Action (Loom-style MVP)**

If Seth isn't comfortable on camera yet, a Loom-style screen recording is a legitimate MVP. Show:
- A Telegram conversation with OpenClaw doing something impressive
- Browser control (Claude controlling Chrome to do research)
- A real-world task completed end-to-end

This is fast to produce (1 hour of work), costs nothing, and proves the product is real. It's not the ideal long-term play but it's infinitely better than no video.

---

**❌ Animated Explainer — Skip for Now**
- Expensive ($2,000–$10,000+ for quality work)
- Takes 4–8 weeks
- Often feels generic for a personal brand product
- Doesn't build trust with the founder — it builds brand awareness

Consider this later as a secondary asset, not the primary hero video.

**❌ Client Testimonial Compilation — Not Yet**
- Great when you have 5–10 happy clients with story arcs
- Right now the evidence suggests early-stage; compile testimonials but use them as supporting proof, not the primary video

## 3. Optimal Video Length

| Placement | Recommended Length |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Hero (above fold) | **45–75 seconds** |
| "How It Works" section | **90–120 seconds** |
| Near pricing | **30–45 seconds** (brief "here's what you get") |
| YouTube/organic content | **3–8 minutes** (separate from landing page) |

**Rule:** Front-load the value. Assume half your visitors won't make it past 30 seconds. The first 10 seconds must hook them. The CTA must be clear by second 45.

For ClawHQ's MVP: **Target 60 seconds.** Long enough to demonstrate the product. Short enough to hold attention.

## 4. Autoplay (Muted) vs Click-to-Play?

### Verdict: **Click-to-play with a strong thumbnail**

| | Autoplay (Muted) | Click-to-Play |
|--|--|--|
| View count | Higher | Lower |
| Lead quality | Lower | Higher |
| Intent signal | Weak | Strong |
| Conversion per viewer | Lower | Higher |
| Annoyance risk | Medium | None |
| Mobile UX | Can be jarring | Clean |

At $349–997, **you want high-intent viewers**, not passive scroll-by impressions. Click-to-play self-selects for people who actually want to understand what they're buying.

**Thumbnail tip:** Use a frame with Seth's face looking direct-to-camera with a slight smile, and overlay text like: *"Watch: What OpenClaw does in 60 seconds"* or *"See it run live →"*

The only exception: a **very short (5–8s) looping muted video** in the hero background as a visual element (showing terminal output, Telegram messages flying in, etc.) — that's fine and won't hurt conversions. But the main explainer should be click-to-play.

**Why hero placement?** The product is invisible and the concept is unfamiliar. Visitors need to understand *what they're buying* before they'll even scroll down. The video does that work faster than any paragraph of text.

## 6. Can Loom or Screen Recordings Work as MVP?

**Yes. Absolutely.** Here's the case:

Loom is designed for exactly this kind of "watch me show you something" communication. For a technical service sold to early adopters, a genuine Loom recording can outperform polished video because:

1. **It's authentic** — doesn't look AI-generated or stock
2. **It builds personal trust** — the founder is right there, talking to you
3. **It proves the product is real** — a screen recording can't be faked easily
4. **Zero production cost** — Seth can record it tonight

**Loom-specific tips:**
- Turn on captions (viewers often watch muted)
- Use picture-in-picture: face + screen simultaneously
- Edit the thumbnail in Loom dashboard to use a good face shot
- Keep it under 90 seconds
- End with a verbal CTA that matches the page button

**The real answer:** A genuine Loom from Seth recorded in one take is 10x better than no video, and possibly better than a scripted "polished" video that feels stiff. Ship the Loom. Upgrade later.

## 7. GIF/Animation as Middle Ground?

### Use case for ClawHQ: **Yes, as supporting elements — not primary**

GIFs are perfect for:
- Showing a specific OpenClaw interaction without requiring a full video play
- The "How It Works" section (animated Telegram conversation)
- Terminal boot-up sequence — this is actually a great idea for ClawHQ's aesthetic
- Social media content

**However:**
- GIFs are often *larger file size* than equivalent MP4/WebM — use `<video autoplay loop muted playsinline>` with an MP4 instead of actual GIF format
- A GIF doesn't build trust like a face does — it can demonstrate but it can't sell

**ClawHQ-specific idea:**
```
[Animated terminal showing]
$ openclaw install
✓ Connecting to server...
✓ Configuring AI model...
✓ Linking Telegram bot...
✓ OpenClaw is live. Say hello in Telegram.
```
This as a looping hero background element (subtle, dark, moody) while headline and CTA sit in front — that's a compelling visual that communicates the product is technical and real without requiring a click.

## 8. YouTube Embed vs Self-Hosted

### Verdict: **YouTube for MVP, consider Vimeo or Bunny.net later**

| | YouTube | Vimeo Pro | Bunny.net / Self-hosted CDN |
|--|--|--|--|
| Speed (w/ lazy loading) | Good | Good | Best |
| Branding control | None | Full | Full |
| Page distraction (suggested videos) | Yes ❌ | No ✅ | No ✅ |
| Cost | Free | $12–20/mo | ~$1–5/mo |
| SEO boost | Ranks in YouTube search | Minimal | Minimal |
| Best for | MVP, organic discovery | Sales pages | Scale |

**Recommendation for ClawHQ:**

1. **Now (MVP):** YouTube with lazy loading (use `youtube-nocookie.com` domain + `loading="lazy"` attribute). Free, fast enough, gets the video indexed.

2. **Once converting:** Switch to **Vimeo Pro** ($12/mo) or **Bunny.net** (~$1–5/mo). You get rid of YouTube's "related videos" at the end (which can pull buyers off your page) and you control the player branding.

3. **Key technical tip:** Never embed YouTube with a standard `<iframe>` that loads on page load. Use a facade pattern — show a thumbnail image, load the actual iframe only when the user clicks play. This alone can save 400–700ms of load time.

## 9. What Do "Invisible Product" Companies Do for Video?

### Successful patterns from SaaS, hosting, and backend tools:

**Stripe:** No traditional hero video. Uses subtle animated card/payment UI loops. But their product isn't truly invisible — it has visual APIs. Key insight: they show the *output* (a payment succeeding) not the infrastructure.

**Cloudflare:** Uses short explainer animations showing "before/after" traffic flow diagrams — not product UI. The visual metaphor replaces the absent UI.

**Zapier:** Famous for screen recordings of workflow automation being set up. But they also lean heavily on "outcome" language — "When this happens → Then do this." The outcome IS the product.

**Notion AI / similar tools:** Heavy use of screen recordings showing the product in a real workflow, plus testimonials from real users showing measurable time savings.

**The pattern for invisible products:**

1. **Show the output, not the process** — Don't show the server. Show the Telegram message that appears. Show the task that got done while Seth was sleeping.

2. **Use before/after** — "Before: Seth manually checking 7 apps. After: One Telegram message."

3. **Lean on testimonials** — If customers love it, their words carry the video when the product can't show itself visually.

4. **Use animation to show system flow** — A simple diagram showing: `You → Telegram → OpenClaw → [Browser / Spreadsheet / Calendar / Whatever]` is more powerful than trying to show a server.

**Directly applicable to OpenClaw:** The best "invisible product" video shows the *moment of delight* — Seth sends a Telegram message, the AI does something amazing, and Seth reacts genuinely. That reaction shot is worth more than any polished product demo.

## 10. 60-Second MVP Video Script (Record on Phone Tonight)

**Format:** Face-to-camera intro → Screen recording → Face-to-camera close  
**Setting:** Casual but intentional. Good light (window light). Clean background.  
**Energy:** Calm confidence. Not a pitch. More like "let me just show you this thing."

---

### SCRIPT OUTLINE

**[0:00–0:10] — Hook / Identification**
> *[Face to camera]*
> "If you've ever wished you had an assistant that actually knew your business, ran 24/7, and you could just message on Telegram — that's exactly what OpenClaw is."

---

**[0:10–0:20] — What It Is (One Sentence)**
> *[Still face to camera, or cut to phone showing Telegram]*
> "I install a personal AI assistant on a private server. It's connected to your Telegram. And it can browse the web, control apps, run research, and do actual work — not just answer questions."

---

**[0:20–0:45] — Live Demo (Screen Recording)**
> *[Switch to phone screen recording of Telegram chat — or desktop Telegram]*
> *[Show Seth typing a message and OpenClaw responding with something impressive]*
> *[Voice over or text overlay describing what's happening]*
> 
> Example: "Watch — I ask it to find me the top 5 Tucson properties listed in the last 48 hours under $300k, and summarize them."
> *[Show the response appearing — detailed, real, useful]*
> "That just saved me 20 minutes."

---

**[0:45–0:55] — Offer + Credibility**
> *[Back to face to camera]*
> "Setup runs $349 to $997 depending on how customized you want it. I handle everything. You're up and running in under a week."
> "This is what I use myself, every day."

---

**[0:55–1:00] — CTA**
> *[Face to camera, direct]*
> "If you want your own — there's a button below this video. Let's talk."

---

**Production notes:**
- **Film vertically or horizontally** — either works, just be consistent
- **Good natural light** beats any ring light setup
- **One take is fine** — authenticity > perfection at this stage
- **Add captions** in Loom, CapCut, or YouTube auto-captions
- **No need for B-roll, music, or graphics** — straight talk + screen share is enough for MVP

## Key Takeaways

1. **Video will likely help ClawHQ** — the product is invisible, the price is high, and trust is the barrier. All three are solved by video.

2. **Founder face + screen hybrid is the winning format** — not animated explainer, not pure screen recording.

3. **Ship the Loom first.** Don't wait for a "real" production video. A genuine 60-second Loom from Seth is the highest-ROI marketing action available right now.

4. **Click-to-play, not autoplay** — at this price point, you want self-selected buyers.

5. **Hero section placement** — don't bury it. The product needs explaining before visitors will scroll.

6. **YouTube is fine to start.** Switch to Vimeo Pro once the page is converting.

7. **Competitor has no video** — this is a first-mover advantage. Do it.
